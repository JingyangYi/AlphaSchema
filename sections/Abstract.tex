Automated alpha mining has recently shifted from operator- and formula-based search toward large language model (LLM) agents. However, many LLM-based systems lack an explicitly defined and learnable hypothesis space, relying on agents to generate,memorize, reflect, and propose new search
directions. This entangles semantic exploration with code generation and makes search feedback difficult to learn and reuse. We introduce \method{}, which reformulates alpha mining as exploration over an explicit space of trading semantics. Each hypothesis is represented by a structured schema comprising \emph{Event}, \emph{Context}, \emph{Qualities}, \emph{Direction}, and \emph{Output}. \method{} decouples exploration from realization: an LLM translates selected schemas into leakage-checked executable factor code, while exploration is learned from evaluated schema--reward pairs and performed by a transparent selector over promising, uncertain, novel, and locally mutated hypotheses.Experiments on CSI300 show that \method{} discovers factor pools with strong predictive and portfolio performance. Schema ablations and reward-prediction analyses further demonstrate that the semantic space is both informative and learnable. Across six heterogeneous LLM
backends, successfully realized factors retain comparable signal quality, indicating that \method{} is largely backend-agnostic and that backend choice mainly affects implementation reliability rather than alpha quality.
